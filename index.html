Trading SPX with a Gamma-Exposure Map

This trainer teaches one core idea: the biggest non-directional player in SPX — the options dealers — are forced to hedge, and their hedging changes how price moves. If you know their position (roughly), you know when the market tends to be sticky and when it tends to slide. Everything in the Practice Sim and Case Studies is built on the mechanics below. You can read this first or just start trading — the Coach will teach you as you go.

1. Who are the dealers, and why must they hedge?

When you buy an SPX call, a market maker (a “dealer”) usually sells it to you. Dealers don’t want directional risk — their business is collecting spreads, not betting on direction. So they immediately delta-hedge: sell you a call (they’re short delta as the market rises), and they buy futures/stock to stay neutral.

The hedge isn’t static. As SPX moves, the option’s delta changes — and the dealer must keep re-hedging. How fast delta changes is gamma.

2. Gamma decides whether dealers calm the market or shove it

There are only two cases, and they produce opposite market “weather”:

Positive gamma = the market has shock absorbers. Negative gamma = the market has a turbo strapped to whichever direction it’s already going.

3. The GEX map: profile, walls, and the flip

Gamma exposure (GEX) estimates, strike by strike, how much hedging pressure lives at each price. For each option: Γ × Open Interest × 100 × S² × 1% — the dollar amount dealers must trade per 1% SPX move. Add calls as positive and puts as negative (the standard “naive” convention — see lesson 6), and you get the profile you’ll see on every chart in this app:

A GEX-by-strike profile, rotated so strikes run vertically like a price axis (that’s how the sim draws it, aligned with the chart).

Three levels matter most:

Call wall — the strike with the biggest positive gamma, usually above spot. Dealers get longer gamma as price approaches it and sell harder into strength. It acts like resistance / a magnet from below, and it’s where rallies stall on pin days.
Put wall — the biggest negative-gamma strike below spot. It often acts as support, both because dealer hedging of the puts is concentrated there and because it’s where put sellers defend.
Gamma flip (zero-gamma level) — the price where net dealer gamma crosses zero. Above it: stabilizing regime. Below it: amplifying regime. Crossing the flip is a regime change, not just another level break — the same 20-point move behaves completely differently on each side.

4. The two-regime playbook you’ll be graded on

The Coach grades your process, not just your P&L. Its rubric is this playbook:

In positive gamma: the tape mean-reverts. Buying calls right under the call wall is the classic mistake — you’re paying premium exactly where the dealer sell-wall lives. Higher-quality trades: fade moves into walls, or if you must own premium, own it near the middle of the range, take profits fast (50–100% on 0DTE is a win), and remember theta is your enemy all day because the market isn’t going anywhere.

In negative gamma: the tape trends. Fading a move because it “looks extended” fights dealer flows that are pushing it further. Higher-quality trades: go with the move after the flip breaks, use the flip as your invalidation, and size down — realized vol might be 3–5× a pin day. IV is expensive, so consider debit spreads over naked longs.

Always, on 0DTE: theta decay is brutal and nonlinear — an at-the-money SPX option loses value fastest in the final 90 minutes. Holding OTM lottery tickets into the last half hour is donating. The sim models this burn realistically; feel it.

5. 0DTE option anatomy (what the ticket shows you)

Delta: your directional exposure — a 0.30-delta call gains ≈ $30 per contract per 1-point SPX move (×100 multiplier). Gamma: how fast that delta grows if the move continues — huge on 0DTE near the money. Theta: what you bleed per day just standing still; the sim shows it per-hour because on 0DTE a “day” is the whole life of the trade. IV: the price of the lottery ticket — it gets crushed after events and on pin days, and it spikes on breaks. You can be right on direction and still lose to IV crush + theta. SPX options are cash-settled at the close: no shares, just the difference in dollars.

6. Honesty box: what GEX can and cannot tell you

The standard GEX calculation makes a big simplifying assumption: dealers are long the calls and short the puts (because institutions mostly overwrite calls and buy protective puts). It’s often roughly right on index — but not always. In Feb 2023-style 0DTE call-buying frenzies, dealers were short the near-money calls, so what looked like a “call wall” was actually rocket fuel. The Case Studies include one of those days on purpose.

Other real-world caveats: GEX from open interest is a morning snapshot — intraday 0DTE flow can overwhelm it; vanna and charm (hedging flows from IV changes and time passing) matter too, especially into OPEX; and every vendor (SpotGamma, Menthor Q, GEXBot, etc.) uses slightly different assumptions, so levels differ a few points between them. Treat the map as context, not signals: it tells you the market’s temperament today, which trades are structurally favored, and where the important prices are. It does not tell you what will happen.

This is an educational simulator. Real 0DTE trading involves slippage, liquidity holes, and account-destroying tail risk that no sim fully reproduces. Nothing here is investment advice — it’s a gym.
